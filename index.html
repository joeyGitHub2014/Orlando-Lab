Welcome to the Orlando Lab at Michigan State University. Our laboratory focuses on revealing the structure, conformational dynamics, and function of membrane proteins. We primarily focus on membrane enzymes, transporters, and receptors involved in lipid signaling and cellular detoxification. Our laboratory relies heavily on cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) to obtain insights into the structure and dynamics of these targets. By combining cryo-EM with biochemical, biophysical, and enzymatic approaches we seek to visualize and understand how membrane proteins sense and transport an array of lipids and small molecules.

Research

Bioactive Lipid Synthesis, Transport, and Signaling

Bioactive lipids are a diverse class of signaling molecules including sphingolipids, endocannabinoids, steroids, leukotrienes, and eicosanoids among others. These molecules mediate a wide range of biological processes such as regulation of pain and inflammation, cellular proliferation, synaptic transmission, and cardiac health. Biological processes in tissues across the human body are dependent on bioactive lipid signaling, with these pathways containing numerous drug targets. Some of the most commonly utilized over the counter medications such as Ibuprofen and Naproxen are targeted specifically at regulating bioactive lipid signaling. Our laboratory is focused on understanding the enzymes, transporters, and receptors that power these potent signaling cascades. We place a particular emphasis on providing high resolution 3-dimensional snapshots of membrane proteins involved in bioactive lipid synthesis, transport, and signaling.

Cellular Detoxification and Cytoprotection

Organisms from bacteria to humans have developed systems to protect themselves from environmental stress. A key component to many of these stress response systems is expression of transporters at the cell membrane, which actively pump toxic compounds to the extracellular environment. These detoxification systems are found in various bacteria and fungi, and also in critical human organ systems such as the blood brain barrier and lining of the digestive system. Although these transport systems often play an important role in maintining cellular health and preventing accumulation of toxic compounds, their presence can also lead to undesirable consequences for human health. Several microorganisms have developed transport systems that provide a formidable defense against our most powerful antibiotics. Expression of certain transporters poses a significant hurdle to chemotherapy and absorption of compounds in the human body. We utilize structural and functional approaches to understand the mechanism of these important transporters at the molecular level.
